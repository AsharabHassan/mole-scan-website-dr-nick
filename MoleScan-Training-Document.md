# MoleScan — Complete Training Document

## Company Overview

MoleScan is a clinician-led mole and skin lesion assessment platform operated by Dermme Health Ltd, a company registered in England and Wales. The platform is designed for healthcare professionals and non-dermatology practitioners across the United Kingdom who encounter moles and skin lesions in their daily work.

MoleScan is not a medical device. It is a clinical workflow and triage platform. It combines AI-powered image analysis with UK consultant dermatologist review to deliver structured skin lesion assessment reports within 24 hours. The final clinical decision always rests with the treating clinician or practitioner.

The platform serves two distinct markets: private clinics and aesthetic practitioners, and the NHS and Integrated Care Boards (ICBs).

---

## What Problem Does MoleScan Solve?

### The Core Problem

Skin cancer is one of the most common cancers in the United Kingdom. Early detection of melanoma dramatically improves survival rates. However, the vast majority of skin lesions are benign. The challenge lies in determining which lesions require urgent attention and which can be safely monitored or reassured.

For NHS primary care, this creates a bottleneck. Approximately 95% of urgent skin cancer referrals through the 2-week-wait pathway turn out to be benign lesions. This puts enormous pressure on dermatology departments, creates long waiting times for patients, and wastes NHS resources. Non-urgent dermatology appointments can take 6 or more weeks.

For non-dermatology practitioners such as beauticians, aesthetic therapists, podiatrists, laser technicians, and chiropractors, the problem is different but equally pressing. These practitioners work on or near skin every day. When they encounter a mole or skin lesion in a treatment area, they face a difficult choice: treat over or around it and accept the risk, cancel or delay the treatment, or tell the client to go see their GP, which may take weeks.

### The MoleScan Solution

MoleScan provides a structured pathway that allows practitioners to capture a dermoscopic image of the mole or skin lesion, upload it to the MoleScan platform, have it reviewed by a UK consultant dermatologist, and receive a clear, actionable report within 24 hours.

The report uses a RAG-rated system:
- GREEN means low risk. The practitioner can reassure the client or patient and, where appropriate, proceed with treatment.
- AMBER means intermediate risk. The lesion should be monitored or reviewed. A possible referral may be recommended.
- RED means high risk. An urgent referral or fast-track pathway is recommended.

This system gives practitioners who are not dermatologists a professional safety net. They do not need to diagnose the lesion themselves. They simply capture the image and upload it. The expert handles the clinical assessment.

## How MoleScan Works — The 10-Step Process

The full MoleScan assessment process, as used in aesthetic and allied health clinic settings, follows these steps:

### Step 1: Client Attends the Clinic
A client arrives at an aesthetic clinic, beauty salon, podiatry clinic, laser clinic, or similar practice for a scheduled treatment.

### Step 2: Practitioner Notices a Mole in the Treatment Area
During the appointment, the practitioner notices a mole or skin lesion in or near the treatment zone. This could be on the face before Botox, on the leg before laser hair removal, under a toenail during a podiatry session, or on the back during a massage.

### Step 3: Practitioner Recommends MoleScan Assessment Before Treatment
Rather than ignoring the mole, treating over it, or cancelling the appointment, the practitioner recommends a MoleScan assessment. This is the "Pause. Assess. Then treat." principle. Not every lesion should be treated without assessment.

### Step 4: Questions Answered and Dermoscopic Images Captured
The practitioner answers a set of pre-determined clinical questions about the lesion (location, size, any changes the client has noticed, relevant history) and captures dermoscopic images using a dermoscope attachment on their smartphone.

### Step 5: Case Uploaded to MoleScan
The practitioner uploads the dermoscopic images and clinical information to the MoleScan platform. This takes under 2 minutes. All data is encrypted and stored securely within the United Kingdom.

### Step 6: Case Prepared for Clinician-Led Review
MoleScan's clinical team reviews the submitted case. AI-assisted analysis supports the process by flagging features of concern and providing initial risk stratification. This helps prioritise cases and supports the reporting dermatologist.

### Step 7: Case Reviewed by UK Dermatologist
Every single case is reviewed by a UK GPwSI (GP with Special Interest) in dermatology or a consultant dermatologist. This is what makes MoleScan clinician-led: human expertise validates every assessment. No clinical output is ever delivered without clinician review.

### Step 8: Final Report Sent to Clinic Within 24 Hours
The clinic receives a comprehensive, structured report within 24 hours of submission. The report includes the dermatologist's clinical assessment, the RAG-rated outcome (GREEN, AMBER, or RED), and a clear recommendation for next steps.

### Step 9: Outcome Guidance (RAG-Rated)
The report provides clear guidance:
- GREEN means low risk. Reassure the client. Monitor if appropriate.
- AMBER means intermediate risk. Review the lesion. Monitor it. A possible referral may be needed.
- RED means high risk. Urgent referral recommended. Fast-track the client to their GP or specialist.

### Step 10: Clinic Decides the Appropriate Action
Armed with the MoleScan report, the clinic decides what to do next:
- If GREEN: Proceed with the planned treatment.
- If AMBER: Reassess. Monitor. Schedule a follow-up.
- If RED: Refer the client to their GP or specialist. Do not proceed with treatment in the lesion area.

The result is safe, confident care for clients. Better outcomes. Stronger trust between practitioner and client.

The guiding principle is: "Check first. Treat with confidence." MoleScan supports clinical excellence and client safety.

## Who Uses MoleScan?

### Aesthetic Clinics
Treatments such as Botox, dermal fillers, chemical peels, and microneedling often require working directly on the face or body where moles may be present. A mole sitting where a practitioner needs to inject or apply treatment cannot be safely treated over without knowing it is safe. MoleScan allows aesthetic clinics to get a dermatologist's assessment within 24 hours so they can proceed with confidence or advise the client appropriately.

### Beauty Therapists
Facials, waxing, microdermabrasion, threading, and other beauty treatments bring practitioners into close contact with clients' skin. During a facial or waxing appointment, a beauty therapist may spot a mole they have not seen before, or one that looks different from the last visit. They cannot just ignore it. MoleScan lets them flag it professionally without needing any clinical training. The client sees that the therapist cares about their safety, which builds trust and loyalty.

### Laser and IPL Clinics
Laser hair removal, skin rejuvenation, and IPL treatments carry a specific risk around moles. Lasering over an unassessed mole is a serious safety concern. Practitioners must avoid moles during treatment, but they need to understand why a mole is there and whether it is safe. A MoleScan report either clears the mole or flags it for referral, allowing the practitioner to map their treatment area safely and proceed with the rest of the session.

### Podiatry Clinics
Podiatrists encounter skin lesions on the feet and toes during routine treatments, verruca removal, and biomechanical assessments. A dark lesion on the foot or under a toenail can look like a bruise, a verruca, or a subungual melanoma. Podiatrists need to rule out the worst before treating. MoleScan gives them a fast expert opinion on foot and nail lesions. This is particularly important because acral melanoma (melanoma on the extremities) is one of the most commonly missed forms of skin cancer.

### Tattoo Removal Studios
Laser tattoo removal involves treating large areas of skin with intense laser energy. Tattoo ink can obscure moles or hide changes in pigmentation. Before lasering a tattooed area, any moles within or near the tattoo must be assessed. MoleScan helps tattoo removal practitioners identify and assess moles in tattooed skin so they can proceed with removal safely and avoid areas that need clinical attention.

### Chiropractors and Massage Therapists
Chiropractors and massage therapists work on exposed skin daily, particularly on the back, shoulders, and neck. They may notice a mole on a client's back that the client has never seen themselves — raised, irregular, or changing in appearance. Flagging it through MoleScan turns a casual observation into a professional duty of care. This could make a life-changing difference for the client, as back melanomas are often detected late because people cannot easily see their own backs.

### Private GP Clinics
Private GPs use MoleScan to support their skin lesion assessment pathway. When patients present with suspicious moles, GPs can submit dermoscopic images through MoleScan and receive a dermatologist-reviewed report within 24 hours. This is faster than NHS referral pathways and gives patients timely answers.

### NHS Primary Care and ICBs
MoleScan sits between primary care and secondary care in the NHS skin cancer referral pathway. GPs use MoleScan to triage skin lesions before making a 2-week-wait referral. Cases assessed as benign can be safely managed in primary care, while suspicious cases are flagged for urgent referral with supporting clinical evidence. This reduces unnecessary pressure on dermatology departments.

## The Clinical Process in Detail

### Image Capture Requirements
MoleScan requires dermoscopic images for all submissions. A dermoscope is a specialised magnifying device that allows practitioners to see structures in the skin that are not visible to the naked eye. Dermoscope attachments for smartphones are widely available and affordable. MoleScan provides image quality guidance within the platform to help practitioners capture optimal images.

Standard photographs taken with a regular smartphone camera are not sufficient for MoleScan assessment. The dermoscopic detail is essential for accurate clinical assessment and reliable triage.

### Clinical Information Required
When submitting a case, practitioners provide:
- The client or patient's age
- The location of the lesion on the body
- Relevant clinical history
- Any symptoms the client has reported, such as changes in size, shape, colour, itching, or bleeding
- Whether the lesion is new or has been present for some time

### The Clinical Triage Layer
Before the dermatologist reviews the case, MoleScan's clinical team performs an initial triage. This includes AI-assisted analysis that flags features of concern and provides preliminary risk stratification. This triage layer does not replace the dermatologist review — it supports it. It helps prioritise cases that need closer attention and ensures consistency across all submissions.

### Dermatologist Review
Every single case submitted to MoleScan is reviewed by a UK-based clinician. These are either GPs with a Special Interest in dermatology (GPwSI) or consultant dermatologists. All reviewing clinicians are registered with the General Medical Council (GMC), and dermatologists hold current specialist registration in dermatology. All clinicians undergo credential verification before joining the platform.

The dermatologist examines the dermoscopic images, considers the clinical context provided, and makes a clinical assessment. This assessment forms the basis of the structured report.

### The Structured Report
Every MoleScan report follows a standardised format to ensure consistency and completeness. The report includes:
- The dermoscopic images submitted
- The clinical information provided
- The dermatologist's clinical assessment
- A RAG-rated outcome (GREEN, AMBER, or RED)
- A clear recommendation for next steps
- A timestamp and audit trail

Reports are designed to be actionable. Each one concludes with a clear outcome: reassure, monitor, or refer urgently.

## Clinical Governance and Safety

### Clinical Safety Standards
MoleScan's clinical safety framework is aligned with DCB0129 (manufacturer clinical safety) and DCB0160 (deploying organisation clinical safety) standards. These are the NHS standards for clinical risk management of health IT systems. A formal Clinical Safety Case is maintained and reviewed regularly.

MoleScan maintains a designated Clinical Safety Officer (CSO) who is responsible for overseeing the clinical safety case, hazard management, and incident reporting processes.

### Clinical Incident Management
MoleScan maintains a clinical incident reporting and management process aligned with DCB0129. All incidents are reviewed by the Clinical Safety Officer, and findings are fed into the clinical safety case and hazard log. Serious incidents are escalated in accordance with regulatory requirements.

### Data Protection and Privacy
MoleScan is fully compliant with the UK General Data Protection Regulation (UK GDPR) and the Data Protection Act 2018. Dermme Health Ltd is registered with the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO).

Key data protection measures include:
- All patient and client data is stored within the United Kingdom. No data is transferred outside the UK.
- All data is encrypted in transit using TLS 1.2 or higher and at rest using AES-256 encryption.
- Access to patient data is strictly controlled through role-based access controls.
- MoleScan collects only the minimum data necessary for clinical assessment (data minimisation principle).
- Data retention policies are transparent and documented.

### Quality Assurance
Every MoleScan report follows a standardised format ensuring consistency and completeness. A complete audit trail is maintained for every assessment, including image capture, clinical review, and report delivery timestamps. MoleScan monitors clinical outcomes and assessment quality through regular clinical audit cycles, feeding findings back into platform improvements.

### Accessibility and Standards
The MoleScan platform and website are built to Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.2 Level AA standards. MoleScan is designed with reference to the NHS Service Standard, supporting usability, accessibility, and clinical safety requirements. The platform is designed to align with the Digital Technology Assessment Criteria (DTAC).

### Patient Consent
Clinicians and practitioners must obtain patient or client consent before capturing and uploading images to MoleScan. The platform provides consent workflow support and documentation to help clinics maintain compliant consent processes.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### General Questions

**I am not a doctor. Can I still use MoleScan?**
Absolutely. MoleScan is specifically designed for non-dermatology practitioners like aesthetic therapists, beauticians, podiatrists, and laser technicians. You capture the image. A UK dermatologist does the clinical assessment. No medical training is required on your end.

**Do I need a dermoscope?**
Yes. MoleScan works with dermoscopic images to ensure the highest quality assessment. A dermoscope attachment for your smartphone is all you need. MoleScan can advise on compatible devices.

**How quickly will I get an answer?**
Within 24 hours. You will receive a clear RAG-rated report: GREEN (safe to proceed with treatment), AMBER (monitor or review needed), or RED (urgent referral recommended).

**What happens if the mole is flagged as concerning?**
You will receive guidance in the report. For AMBER or RED results, the report recommends next steps, typically advising the client to see their GP or a specialist. You are not expected to manage the clinical pathway, just to act on the guidance.

**Can I charge clients for the mole check?**
Yes. Many clinics offer MoleScan as an add-on service or include it as part of their duty of care. It can become a revenue stream while demonstrating professionalism and client safety.

**Is my client's data secure?**
Yes. MoleScan is fully GDPR compliant with all data encrypted and stored securely within the United Kingdom.

### Clinical Questions

**How is MoleScan different from a teledermatology service?**
Traditional teledermatology sends images to a dermatologist for review. MoleScan adds a structured clinical triage layer that provides risk stratification before the dermatologist review. This combination of expert triage and dermatologist oversight enables faster, more consistent assessments while maintaining the clinical rigour of human expert review.

**Can MoleScan detect melanoma?**
MoleScan is not a diagnostic tool. It is a triage platform that helps clinicians and practitioners assess whether a skin lesion requires urgent referral, monitoring, or reassurance. Final diagnosis requires histopathological confirmation following biopsy where indicated.

**Is patient consent required?**
Yes. Practitioners must obtain client or patient consent before capturing and uploading images to MoleScan. The platform provides consent workflow support and documentation.

**Is MoleScan a registered medical device?**
MoleScan is a clinical workflow and triage platform that supports clinician decision-making. It is not currently classified or registered as a standalone medical device. The platform provides structured clinical triage with UK GP and dermatologist review, with the final clinical decision always resting with the treating clinician.

**How are the dermatologists vetted?**
All MoleScan GPs and dermatologists are registered with the General Medical Council (GMC), and dermatologists hold current specialist registration in dermatology. All clinicians undergo credential verification before joining the platform.

### NHS Questions

**How does MoleScan fit into existing NHS referral pathways?**
MoleScan sits between primary care and secondary care in the skin cancer referral pathway. GPs and primary care clinicians use MoleScan to triage lesions before making a 2-week-wait referral. Cases assessed as benign can be safely managed in primary care, while suspicious cases are flagged for urgent referral with supporting clinical evidence.

**Can MoleScan be deployed across multiple practices within an ICB?**
Yes. MoleScan is designed for multi-site deployment. ICBs can commission MoleScan across multiple GP practices and primary care centres, with centralised reporting and governance oversight.

MoleScan's approach combines two established clinical methodologies: structured dermoscopic image assessment for risk stratification, and expert dermatologist teledermatology review. Both methods have strong evidence bases in the published literature.

## Glossary

- **Dermoscopy**: A technique using a specialised magnifying device (dermoscope) to examine skin lesions in detail, revealing structures below the skin surface invisible to the naked eye.
- **RAG Rating**: Red, Amber, Green classification system used to communicate risk level. Red means high risk/urgent. Amber means intermediate/monitor. Green means low risk/reassure.
- **2WW (Two-Week Wait)**: An NHS urgent referral pathway where patients suspected of having cancer must be seen by a specialist within two weeks of referral.
- **ICB (Integrated Care Board)**: NHS organisations responsible for planning and commissioning healthcare services in their area.
- **GPwSI**: General Practitioner with a Special Interest, typically in dermatology. A GP who has additional training and expertise in a specific clinical area.
- **DCB0129**: NHS standard for clinical risk management applied to manufacturers of health IT systems.
- **DCB0160**: NHS standard for clinical risk management applied to organisations deploying health IT systems.
- **DTAC**: Digital Technology Assessment Criteria, a set of baseline standards for digital health technologies used in the NHS.
- **Subungual Melanoma**: A type of melanoma that occurs under the fingernails or toenails, often appearing as a dark stripe or discolouration.
- **Acral Melanoma**: Melanoma occurring on the palms, soles of feet, or under nails. Often diagnosed late because it occurs in areas people do not regularly check.
- **Teledermatology**: The practice of dermatology using telecommunications technology, typically involving the review of clinical images by a remote dermatologist.
- **Triage**: The process of determining the priority of treatment based on the severity of a condition. In MoleScan's context, sorting skin lesions into reassure, monitor, or refer urgently categories.
